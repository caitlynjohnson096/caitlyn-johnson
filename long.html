Overview

A project completed for The Stanford Longevity Design Challenge with a team of students from Design Consulting at Cornell. We designed a gardening app to encourage urban dwellers interact with their physical environment.

Role: UX Designer

Team: Varsha Iyer, Vivian Chen, Christina Jin, Jade Eggleston

Timeline: September 2021 - November 2021

Tools: Figma, Whimsical, Notion

Problem

How can we motivate and educate urban dwellers on gardening so that they are able to interact with their physical space thus promoting a healthier lifestyle?

Solution

An gardening app that utilizes daily to-dos, check-ins, plant growing in a virtual space, and personalized plant kits to help bridge knowledge gaps and motivate the user to grow a plant successfully.

User Research

Survey

A survey gauging interest and pain points was sent out across various internet platforms, such as Facebook and Reddit.

User Interviews

Interviews were conducted to better understand how people interact with their environment and how gardening may change that.

Synthesis

Our findings were sorted into an affinity diagram to categorize user interests and needs.

Survey Findings

From the survey we were able to conclude that 94% of our participants had a high interest in attempting to garden or had gardened in the past. Additionally, we were able to conclude that 39.9% of participants ranked time as one of their top pain points with space and lack of knowledge making up 23% and 16.9% of participants respectively.

User Interviews

Who are the interviewees:

  • Those who live in urban/suburban areas
  • Those with gardening experience
  • Those who may not have had the opportunity to garden

What are the goals of the interviews:

  • Understand how urban/suburban lifestyles affect well-being
  • Understand how gardening can affect well-being
  • Determine why those interested in gardening have not started

Synthesis

From the findings from the survey we generated an affinity diagram by grouping user information in several different iterations.Then these categories were recategorized into positive and negative groups.

Key Findings

User Interviews showed that many people viewed gardening as:

  • Source of fresh produce
  • Aesthetically Pleasing
  • Sense of Accomplishment
  • Joy and Relaxation

User Interviews showed that people are hesitant to garden due to:

  • Lack of motivation
  • Difficulty in managing

Our findings reflected problems occurring in urban areas:

  • Less connection to the physical environment
  • Mental health effects

Medium-Fi Iterations

To-Do List Feature

Through user research, it was discovered that one of the pain points of users was lack of time. Thus a to-do list design that would effectively address the tasks the user needed to complete in order to keep their plants healthy was iterated on. The goal was to create an accessible design with laid out tasks that would lower the amount of time the user spent attempting to care for the plant.

First Iteration

This iteration shows the daily to-do and a weekly progress on water, sun, and growth. However, keeping the knowledge gap in mind, this design could cause users to over-water their plant the first day of the week in order to complete their weekly process. Thus this design could result in the user not being able to successfully grow the plant.

Second Iteration

The second iteration shows the daily water, sun, and fertilizer in a more pictorial way. This design worked well to break the individual tasks down by day of the week. However, the knowledge gap still wasn’t fully filled as the tasks for the user might need to be broken down into more specific and descriptive tasks.

Final Design

The final design was the simplest , but provided users with the lowest learning curve because it provided the most information about plant care and laid it out in a way that wouldn’t overcomplicate the steps. This allowed the user to become more knowledgeable and reduce the amount of time it took to take care of the plant.

Charity Donation Feature

During medium-fi, an iteration of the charity donation page was created, which aims to motivate users to grow their plant successfully.

First Iteration

This first version of the charity page uses a drop-down menu for a minimalistic look. It also makes it very intuitive to the user that clicking on the charity means you are choosing it to donate.

Final Design

In the final version, the charities were showcased with more visibility. This provided context for the users as the logos and charity taglines allow the users to briefly view what the charity is about before deciding to learn more about it. This page allowed the user to gather information about the charities at a glance rather than needing to click through each one.

Profile Profile

First Iteration

In the first version, the design was focused on creating an easy landing page for the user to navigate to other pages from . The user needed the ability to buy a new plant and see their garden which was integral to the user experience, and putting that on the profile page would reinforce that need. However, this iteration was not meeting the user’s pain points of lack of time, lack of knowledge, and lack of motivation.

Final Iteration

The profile page was approached from two different angles, which creates quite a differentiation in design iterations. At first, the user’s need for easy access and navigation was tackled; however, upon reflection it made more sense to target the user’s motivation pain point . Thus the design incorporates motivators such as levels rather than easy access links.
